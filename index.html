Supplementary Material

In this section, we show that our approach is incentive compatible, welfare maximizing, and can be computed in polynomial time.

Incentive compatibility

Theorem (Incentive compatibility for dynamic intersections):
For each agent \( i \) for \( i=1,2,\ldots,n \) and \( n = n_1 + n_2 + n_3 + n_4 \) where \( n_j \) represents the number of vehicles on the \( j^\textrm{th} \) arm, bidding \( \boldsymbol{b}_i = \boldsymbol{\zeta}_i \) is the dominant strategy.

Proof.

  1. Use SSA to compute an ordering over all agents. But this can generate false bids: overbids and underbids.
  2. An agent may be assigned to go second due to a higher priority vehicle.
  3. But the latter vehicle may not even conflict with the first. Conversely, agent \( i \) might be asked to go first, risking a collision.
  4. Therefore, it's important to identify conflicting and non-conflicting lane groups. Run SSA only in conflicting groups.
  5. Since conflicting groups are optimized and non-conflicting ones donâ€™t interfere, the auction is incentive-compatible.

Welfare maximization

The next desired property in an optimal auction is welfare maximization, which maximizes the total utility earned by every active agent.

Theorem (Welfare maximization for dynamic intersections):
Social welfare of an auction is defined as \( \sum_i \boldsymbol{\zeta}_i \boldsymbol{\alpha}_i \). Bidding \( \boldsymbol{b}_i = \boldsymbol{\zeta}_i \) maximizes social welfare for every agent.

Proof.

We can show that welfare is maximum for each set of conflicting lane group. We need to show that social welfare of the system is maximized when the welfare of each individual conflicting lane group is maximized. Our proof is based on induction.

Base case: \( n=1 \) reduces to the static auction case.
Inductive hypothesis: Suppose the current system consists of \( n > 1 \) conflicting groups and the social welfare is maximized.
Now add \( \lvert n+1 \rvert \) agents from the \( (n+1)^\textrm{th} \) group. The welfare of system with \( n \) conflicting groups is:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^{\lvert i \rvert} \zeta^i_j \alpha^i_j \]

From the hypothesis, the above is maximum. If the \( (n+1)^\textrm{th} \) conflicting group is added and optimized using SSA, the resulting total remains a sum of optimal terms. Hence, welfare is maximized.

Overflow prevention

We propose a strategy to address overflow by transferring a portion of the higher priority agent's bid to the lower priority agent \( i \).

Theorem (Overflow prevention):
In a current SSA, suppose there exists \( (i,j) \in [n_k] \times [n_k], k=1,2,3,4 \), such that \( p_i > p_j \) and \( s_i[t] > s_j[t] \). Let: \[ \boldsymbol{\hat{\zeta}}_i, \boldsymbol{\hat{\zeta}}_j = \left( \boldsymbol{\zeta}_i \xrightarrow{q} \boldsymbol{\zeta}_j \right) \] If: \[ q < \boldsymbol{\zeta}_i \left( 1 - \frac{\alpha_{i+m}}{\alpha_i} \right) - \sum_{s=i}^{i+m-1} \boldsymbol{\zeta}_{s+1} \left( \frac{\alpha_s - \alpha_{s+1}}{\alpha_i} \right) \] then the new SSA with these modified bids is incentive compatible.

Proof.

The difference in utility for agent \( i \) after transferring some valuation to agent \( j \), assuming a jump of \( m \), is:

\[ \Delta u = (v_k - q)\alpha_k - \left[ v_{k+1}(\alpha_k - \alpha_{k+1}) + v_{k+2}(\alpha_{k+1} - \alpha_{k+2}) + \ldots + v_{k+m}(\alpha_{k+m-1} - \alpha_{k+m}) \right] - v_k \alpha_{k+m} \]

Rewriting, \( \Delta u > 0 \) if:

\[ v_k(\alpha_k - \alpha_{k+m}) - \sum_{j=k}^{k+m-1} v_{j+1}(\alpha_j - \alpha_{j+1}) > q\alpha_k \] \[ \Rightarrow q < v_k\left(1 - \frac{\alpha_{k+m}}{\alpha_k} \right) - \frac{1}{\alpha_k} \sum_{j=k}^{k+m-1} v_{j+1}(\alpha_j - \alpha_{j+1}) \]
